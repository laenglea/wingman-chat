## Shape the page around the content

Before writing CSS, decide the page's audience, one-sentence thesis, strongest evidence, and desired
action. Derive the art direction from the selected style and the subject's real visual world. Choose a
field/ink/signal palette, type roles, and one subject-specific signature; do not default to navy,
white-and-gray, rounded cards, or a consulting template unless the material genuinely calls for it.

Every page needs:

- an insight headline and concise context;
- one dominant thesis, finding, or question;
- traceable evidence: a chart, comparison, annotated image/object, map, framework, or a small set of
  genuinely related measures;
- compact source attribution near the evidence;
- a recommendation, implication, or next action only when the source and audience support one.

Choose the composition from the evidence rather than filling required bands:

- **Analytical field:** one large chart or comparison with directly annotated drivers and exceptions.
- **Annotated subject:** a real/imported or generated image, map, product state, or object carries the
  page; labels and evidence orbit it.
- **Decision page:** options, criteria, tradeoffs, and the requested decision form one clear argument.
- **Framework/process:** a spatial model or flow dominates, with risks/owners attached to the relevant
  steps rather than collected in generic side cards.
- **Editorial brief:** a strong typographic thesis, one visual anchor, and short evidence blocks create
  rhythm without pretending every statement is a KPI.

Density follows the audience. A board or operational brief may be compact; a public explainer may need
more breathing room. Do not add a decorative third metric, icon, tag, or action to balance a grid.

## Build

1. Survey the sources and draft the exact headline, thesis, evidence, and citations.
2. Write `styles/theme.css` with reusable field/ink/signal, type, spacing, and layout tokens.
3. Write `slides/slide1.html` as body content only. Its root must be
   `<section class="slide onepager">`; use semantic `<header>`, `<main>`, and `<footer>` where they fit.
4. Use CSS Grid/Flexbox with pinned or bounded regions, `min-width: 0`, and `min-height: 0`. Keep at
   least 80 px outer margin and text at least 24 px on the 1920 × 1080 canvas.
5. If the user supplied a useful image, use `import_image` before generating one. Use
   `generate_image` only for a brief-specific visual anchor; keep critical text and figures in HTML/SVG.
6. Stop when the canvas validator is clean. If it reports overflow or overlap, shorten or remove the
   least important content; do not shrink type below the readable floor or chase subjective polish.

No JavaScript is needed. Keep charts and diagrams as accessible inline SVG with direct labels. The
framework injects the document shell and all `styles/*.css`, so write body content only.
