## Visual Style

Build an art direction that could only belong to this material. Before planning slides, identify:

- the subject's visual world: objects, environments, diagrams, source imagery, textures, or data forms;
- the audience and occasion: board decision, teaching, launch, investigation, proposal, or narrative;
- the emotional register: urgent, precise, optimistic, intimate, monumental, playful, or restrained;
- one recognizable visual signature rooted in the subject, not a generic ornament.

Translate those findings into a coherent system:

- Choose a palette with an **ink**, one or two **fields**, and one meaningful **signal accent**. Pull
  colors from the subject or brand evidence when available; do not default to gray, navy, purple, or
  a dark SaaS palette.
- Choose type roles for the content. Editorial subjects can use a characterful serif display face;
  technical or operational subjects may need a precise grotesk or mono accent; human stories can use
  warmer forms. Use available system-safe fallbacks and keep body text highly readable.
- Let composition follow the evidence: cinematic imagery, annotated objects, maps, diagrams,
  typographic scale, or data marks should carry the story. Do not translate every idea into a card.
- Repeat one subject-derived signature across the deck, such as a map coordinate system, archival
  caption treatment, material edge, measurement notation, crop behavior, or chart annotation style.
- Give the deck rhythm. Alternate immersion and analysis, dense evidence and breathing room, without
  turning the slide list into a checklist of stock templates.

Use generated or imported imagery when the subject benefits from something concrete to inspect.
Critical numbers, labels, and citations remain deterministic HTML/SVG text. The result should feel
specific, authored, and visually memorable while staying faithful to the sources.
